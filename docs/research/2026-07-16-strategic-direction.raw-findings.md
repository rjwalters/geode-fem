**[?] ?**

- A two-level preconditioned Helmholtz-Jacobi-Davidson (PHJD) method is a viable state-of-the-art approach for the algebraic eigenvalue problem from edge-element (H(curl)) discretization of the Maxwell eigenvalue problem, combining Jacobi-Davidson iteration with a Helmholtz projection and Rayleigh-quotient minimization in a small Davidson subspace.
- The method handles the large gradient/near-kernel null-space of the curl-curl operator by solving a Helmholtz projection system together with the preconditioned system in the fine space each iteration, rather than relying solely on an SPD-proxy preconditioner.
- The two-level PHJD method has a provable error-reduction factor gamma = c(H)(1 - C*delta^2/H^2) with C independent of mesh size h and subdomain diameter H, and convergence improves as the number of subdomains grows (c(H) decreases as H->0).
- The preconditioning strategy for the Maxwell eigenproblem uses a two-level domain-decomposition scheme with one coarse-space correction per iteration, a parallel/scalable alternative to shift-invert factorization for edge-element eigenmodes.

- Augmented partial factorization (APF) computes the entire multi-input scattering matrix of a frequency-domain Maxwell system via a single partial factorization / Schur complement, rather than solving Maxwell's equations separately per source.
- For multi-source nanophotonic problems (~10 million variables), APF is reported as 1,000 to 30,000,000 times faster than existing methods.
- The problem addressed is the frequency-domain DRIVEN (linear-solve) EM response, not an eigenvalue problem — it evaluates the full-wave multi-input response directly.
- The method's advantage is largest for multi-channel driven systems (disordered media, aperiodic metasurfaces, densely packed photonic circuits) where each input requires a large-scale simulation.

**[?] ?**

- For interior eigenvalues, shift-and-invert spectral transformation (A-sigma I)^{-1} is the most powerful technique, converting interior eigenvalues to the periphery with good separation, but requires solving a linear system each iteration.
- Harmonic Ritz extraction is a lower-cost alternative to shift-and-invert for interior eigenvalues, with convergence such that eigenvalues closest to the target stabilize first and restarting is safe; it is available in Krylov-Schur, Arnoldi, Generalized Davidson, and Jacobi-Davidson.
- Standard eigensolvers naturally converge peripheral eigenvalues first, so computing interior eigenvalues requires prior convergence of all eigenvalues between them and the periphery - which is why interior problems (like GEODE's transmon interior mode) are intrinsically hard.
- Preconditioned eigensolvers (Generalized Davidson and Jacobi-Davidson) offer an alternative to shift-and-invert when it is infeasible, treating the correction equation as a cheap replacement for the shift-and-invert linear system, solved with a preconditioned iterative solver.
- SLEPc's default eigensolver is Krylov-Schur, and it provides symmetric/Hermitian-optimized methods (Lanczos, RQCG, LOBPCG) plus external preconditioned solvers (BLOPEX, PRIMME) - establishing the production baseline GEODE's matrix-free eigensolver competes against.

- GPU-accelerated matrix-free low-order-refined (LOR) preconditioning extends to H(curl) and H(div) high-order finite element spaces (the electromagnetic/Maxwell-relevant spaces) via a specially constructed interpolation-histopolation basis, covering the full de Rham complex including a large-scale magnetic diffusion problem.
- The LOR preconditioners achieve optimal memory usage and computational complexity for high-order problems, with spectral-equivalence constants independent of mesh size and polynomial degree, enabling scalable matrix-free iterative solves rather than direct factorization.
- The preconditioning approach is built on constructing a spectrally equivalent low-order (assembled/sparse) discretization on a refined mesh, providing a robust preconditioner for matrix-free high-order operators on GPUs.
- The methods demonstrate strong and weak parallel scalability on GPUs, and the relative weighting of algorithmic components differs between GPU and CPU implementations (implying GPU-specific kernel design matters for these matrix-free EM solvers).

**[?] ?**

- The state-of-the-art academic approach for the Maxwell/curl-curl eigenvalue problem with singularities is a preconditioned Jacobi-Davidson (specifically adaptive-multilevel Preconditioned Helmholtz-Jacobi-Davidson, PHJD) method, not shift-invert with an SPD-proxy preconditioner; the multilevel preconditioner is applied directly to the Jacobi-Davidson correction equation.
- The fundamental difficulty in H(curl) curl-curl eigensolvers is the infinite-dimensional kernel of the curl operator: iterative eigen-iterations can collapse into this kernel, which is why a separate Helmholtz/divergence-free projection is required at each step to keep the solution physical.
- The Jacobi-Davidson correction operator becomes ill-conditioned for large-scale discrete Maxwell eigenvalue problems, so an efficient preconditioning technique is essential for the method to remain practical at scale — mirroring GEODE's observation that the preconditioner is the limiter.
- The PHJD method's convergence factor is proven quasi-optimal — independent of mesh size and mesh (refinement) levels — provided the coarse mesh is sufficiently fine, giving mesh-independent convergence for the principal Maxwell eigenmode.
- This method targets the PRINCIPAL (smallest, singular) Maxwell eigenvalue via adaptive edge-element refinement, not a deeply-shifted interior eigenvalue; the singular principal eigenfunction from re-entrant corners is the motivating hard case rather than a deep spectral shift.

- On GPU for high-order H(div) saddle-point problems (p=8, ~1.7M DOF), matrix-free iterative solving crushes direct factorization: ~216x overall speedup, with CG setup ~30,000x faster than Cholesky factorization and the solve ~70x faster than triangular solve.
- Block-preconditioned MINRES for the H(div) saddle-point system converges in an iteration count independent of mesh size and polynomial degree, with a tightly bounded preconditioned condition number (kappa(B^-1 A) <= 2).
- Matrix-free evaluation via sum factorization is essential at high order because matrix nonzeros scale like O(p^{2d}), making explicit assembly infeasible and memory-bound on GPUs.
- A well-designed matrix-free preconditioner can reduce a hard saddle-point block to a tractable form: the approximate Schur complement takes the form of an M-matrix graph Laplacian, enabling highly scalable algebraic multigrid.
- Matrix-free operators on GPU achieve very high throughput for high-order FEM, e.g. peak 3.6x10^9 DOF/second at p=3 for the DG mass inverse via matrix-free CG.

- Assembled sparse-matrix FEM is memory-bandwidth-bound and cannot exceed ~2% arithmetic-unit utilization, whereas matrix-free spectral/high-order element methods reduce cost to O(1) storage and O(p) compute per DoF with very high utilization of modern CPUs and GPUs — quantitative support that matrix-free is a fundamentally better fit for GPU/tensor hardware than assembled operators.

- libCEED ships a first-class Rust interface (alongside Python and Julia), maintained and tested in the main repository — evidence that a Rust-based matrix-free high-order EM solver like GEODE can bind to or align with an established GPU-portable operator ecosystem rather than reinventing the algebra layer.
- The matrix-free advantage grows with polynomial degree: assembled high-order FEM incurs O(p^d) storage and O(p^2d) compute per DoF, making assembly progressively more prohibitive as order increases — quantifying why high-order + matrix-free is the regime where iterative/GPU methods beat direct factorization.
