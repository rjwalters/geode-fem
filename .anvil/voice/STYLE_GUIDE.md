**Purpose**

## 2. Structure and Flow

### Aim For
- A story that moves: hook, insight, tension, resolution.
- Paragraphs that connect through thought, not templates.
- Occasional surprise or change in direction.

### Avoid
- The fixed pattern of introduction, list, and summary.
- Starting every paragraph with "That said," "In other words," or "Ultimately."

## 3. Word Choice

### Prefer
- Concrete nouns and verbs such as *build, cut, shift, hum.*
- Short, plain words instead of inflated ones.
- Personal language (*I, we, you*) when suitable.

### Avoid
- Abstract or inflated words such as *nuance, interplay, emergent, paradigm.*
- Redundant pairs like *complex and nuanced.*
- Empty adjectives like *remarkable* or *truly.*
- Self-flattering adjectives that announce integrity, courage, or intellectual virtue rather than demonstrate it: *honest reversal, honest accounting, honest reading, honest position, candid take, frank assessment, uncomfortable answer, painful truth, humbling admission.* If the move is honest or uncomfortable, the reader will feel that from the move itself. Labeling it is the prose equivalent of "to be honest": a tell that signals AI authorship. Exception: an adjective stays when it does real semantic work (*the hard question* contrasting an easier one; *the actual problem* contrasting a claimed one; *the deeper issue* contrasting a surface one).

## 4. Sentence Rhythm

### Aim For
- Variation in length: a mix of short, medium, and longer sentences.
- Sentences that sound right when spoken aloud.
- Short sentences used *occasionally* for focus or punch.
- Longer sentences that develop a thought, show causation, or connect ideas.

### Avoid
- Chains of clauses joined by punctuation.
- Parentheses that interrupt the flow.
- Perfectly even pacing.
- **Telegraphic, staccato prose**: strings of 3-6 word sentences that read like bullet points converted to fragments.
- **Thesis-statement chains**: three or more consecutive declarative sentences where each one functions as a standalone quotable claim. Even when no single sentence is too short, the chain reads as bullets the writer forgot to bullet. Subordinate one or two sentences into adjacent ones (using *because*, *which*, *so*, or a relative clause); end on substance, not on a final punch line.

## 5. Paragraph Style

### Prefer
- Compact paragraphs of three to six sentences.
- Each paragraph should move the idea forward.
- Transitions that feel natural.

### Avoid
- One-sentence paragraphs used for drama.
- Over-signposted logic such as "This shows that."
- Interchangeable, uniform paragraphs.

---

## 6. Figurative Language

### Use
- Simple metaphors drawn from real experience.
- Clear images tied to the subject.

### Avoid
- Giving human intent to abstract things such as "History wants."
- Explaining a metaphor after writing it.
- Mixing several metaphors in one passage.

**Tip**
Let the image stand on its own. Do not explain your own poetry.

---

## 7. Openings and Closings

### Open With
- A small story, image, or question.
- Something specific and alive.

### Close With
- A tone of reflection or quiet tension.
- No full summary or moral lesson.

**Avoid**
- Generic openings like "Throughout history" or "In today's world."
- Endings that begin with "Ultimately."
- Neat closure that resolves every thought.

---

## 8. Authenticity Checks

Ask before publishing:
1. Does it sound like a person or a press release?
2. Could someone quote a line from it in conversation?
3. Would you stand by this paragraph in a week?
4. Does it include some surprise or risk?
5. Can a reader sense a mind behind the words?

---

## 9. Quick Anti-Tropes Checklist

| Habit | Flag | Fix |
|-------|------|-----|
| "At its core…" | ✗ | Delete or start with an image. |
| "In many ways…" | ✗ | Be direct. |
| Em-dashes (any use is suspect) | ✗ | Default AI-trope tell. Rewrite with comma, colon, period, or relative clause. One per long post is the ceiling, not the target. |
| Sandwich em-dashes (X — Y — Z) | ✗ | Strongest tell of the family. The interjection either belongs in its own sentence, in parentheses, or cut. |
| "Not just X, it is Y" | ✗ | State a fresh idea. |
| Lists of three adjectives | ✗ | Use two or vary the form. |
| "Ultimately," or "In short," | ✗ | Remove. |
| "honest/uncomfortable/candid X" announcing the writer's own virtue | ✗ | Drop the adjective and let the move speak. |
| Thesis-statement chains (3+ punchy declarative sentences in a row) | ✗ | Subordinate one or two with *because/which/so*; close on substance. |
| Too even in rhythm | ✗ | Add one short line. |
| No sensory detail | ✗ | Add one thing seen, heard, or felt. |

## 10. Style Philosophy

Good writing sounds like thinking.
It carries motion, pause, and conviction.
A small rough edge is better than a polished surface with no voice.
