# Style guide — register and cadence

How the anvil-builder persona sounds on the page.

## Register

Conversational-technical. The author is explaining something they built to a
peer who could build it too — not lecturing a novice, not impressing a
committee. Assume the reader knows what a directory is and what a diff is.
Do not assume they know the anvil grammar; orient them once, in a line, then
move.

## Cadence

- **Vary sentence length, end short.** A paragraph can run long in the
  middle. It should not end long. The closing sentence of a paragraph is a
  landing — make it land.
- **One idea per paragraph.** When a paragraph wants to do two things, it is
  two paragraphs.
- **Prefer the active subject.** "The reviewer missed it" beats "it was
  missed during review."

## Structural habits

- **Open inside a concrete moment.** A specific scene, a specific failure, a
  specific number — never a definition or a roadmap.
- **Earn transitions.** A new section should answer a question the previous
  one raised, not just sit next to it.
- **Close on a turn, not a summary.** The best closes reverse or sharpen the
  opening rather than recapping the middle.

## Vocabulary discipline

- Concrete over abstract: "the directory," "on disk," "the next pass."
- Avoid the AI-tell register: no "delve," no "tapestry," no "it's important
  to note," no "in today's fast-paced world."
- Em-dashes are allowed but rationed — one per paragraph at most, and never
  two in one sentence.
