# On knowing when to stop

I used to think the hard part of a review loop was the reviewing. It is not.
The hard part is knowing when to stop.

A scoring rubric gives you a number, and a number feels like permission. Hit
the threshold and you advance; miss it and you revise. Clean. But the loop
has a failure mode the number hides: a draft can climb from 31 to 34 to 36,
clear the bar, and still be the same draft with the same flaw, now wearing
three rounds of polish over it. The score went up. The thing that was wrong
did not move.

So I started watching the spread between passes instead of the totals. When
two consecutive reviews land within a point of each other and neither one
touches the flaw I care about, the loop is not converging — it is stuck, and
the rubric is too coarse to feel it. That is the moment to stop scoring and
read the draft like a person.

The machinery is there to buy attention, not to replace it. When it stops
buying attention, turn it off.
