# One reviewer is usually enough

The instinct when an agent misses something is to add another agent. If one
reviewer let a bad number through, surely two would have caught it. Surely
three.

This is the committee reflex, and it is wrong more often than it is right.
Two reviewers do not give you two independent reads. They give you two reads
that mostly agree, plus the small set where they disagree — and the
disagreement is where your time goes now, adjudicating a tie that neither
critic cared much about. You bought noise and called it rigor.

The cases where a second critic earns its place are narrow and specific: when
the second one is looking at something the first one structurally cannot
see. An auditor checking claims against sources is not a second opinion on
the same prose; it is a different question entirely. That is worth running in
parallel. A second reviewer scoring the same rubric is not.

Add a critic when it asks a new question. Otherwise, one is enough.
