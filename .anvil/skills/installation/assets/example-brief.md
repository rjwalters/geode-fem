## Premise (section 1)

A walk-in spherical anechoic chamber, sunk into the floor of a museum pavilion,
with a small meeting room at its geometric center. Two strangers ascend
opposite spiral paths through an interior of inward-radiating foam wedges. They
meet in the central chamber for exactly sixty seconds in a room that is
acoustically dead and electromagnetically isolated — a space that cannot be
heard, recorded, or transmitted from. They leave by opposite spirals and never
see each other again. The piece is a built rebuttal to a world in which the
conditions that once made privacy possible no longer exist.

## The Frame (section 2)

The constitutional anchor is *Katz v. United States* (1967): the Fourth
Amendment "protects people, not places," and what a person seeks to preserve as
private — even in public — may be constitutionally protected. The expectation
of privacy is created by the steps a person actively takes to construct it. In
1967 those steps (closing a phone-booth door, walking into a field) were free.
In 2026 they are no match for parabolic mics, laser windows, ambient phones,
and the instrumentation of public space. The doctrine survives; the practical
conditions that supported it do not. Quiet Place delivers, as a built object,
what walking into a field used to deliver for free.

## The Visitor's Hour (section 3)

Roughly seven minutes from threshold to re-entry. Shedding (~30 s) → paired
confirmation room (~30 s) → spiral approach in (~3 min) → the encounter (60 s) →
spiral departure out (~3 min) → reclaim (~15 s). The center of gravity is the
approach, not the encounter; the minute is too short for small talk to escape
into it.

## Architecture (section 4)

A thirty-foot matte black sphere, sunk ~20% into the pavilion floor (visual
lineage: Boullée's *Cenotaph for Newton*). Interior lined with inward-radiating
foam "spears" pointing at the geometric center. Two spiral corridors thread
through the spear field at ~5% grade (ADA-friendly), entering at opposite sides
and rising to a central meeting chamber (~8 ft interior diameter, two chairs)
suspended ~9 ft above the floor. The spiral length doubles as a
waveguide-below-cutoff penetration of the Faraday boundary. Spec table: sphere
diameter ~30 ft, spear field ~2 ft, corridor width 4 ft, spiral length ~180 ft,
pavilion ~50×70 ft.

## The Light Language (section 5)

Inside a sealed, sound- and signal-isolated structure, light is the entire
communication layer. One voice: a single warm amber point doing things to time.
A spiral spot guide ticks forward at walking pace (both spirals in lockstep so
the two visitors arrive together). A chamber light runs a 60-second arc.
Spear-tip pixels and an exterior shell ring broadcast state (empty / approaching
/ occupied / departing) — opaque about substance, transparent about phase.

## The Ritual Act — The Shedding (section 6)

The first act of the work: at an entrance counter the visitor places all
recording-capable devices into a numbered airport-style bin, receives a paper
chit, and walks on empty-handed. The bin is mundane on purpose. A Faraday cage
stops transmission but not local capture, so the chamber must be entered without
devices. The empty hand is the gesture. Bins travel by hidden conveyor to a
reclaim counter on the opposite side, only moving once both visitors consent.

## The Consent Structure (section 7)

Pairing is walk-up (arrival order, anti-matching for groups). Before entry the
two strangers are seated facing each other across glass, silent, and each
consents or declines via a small light — without explanation, for any reason.
This is the *Katz* volitional act performed on the partner in front of them. A
surprise/ambush version would invert the anchor (privacy inflicted, not chosen),
so the held look at the glass keeps the work in the right register.

## Safety Without Surveillance (section 8)

Safety designed without violating the no-record promise. Passive sensing only
(door state, occupancy, temperature, CO₂, ambient light — CO₂ is critical in a
sealed room). A baffled passive air exchange (no interior fan). Sixty-second
sessions enforced by the light arc; failsafe-open doors on power loss, panic,
end-of-session, smoke, or CO₂ spike. Two trained staff on duty who can see the
entrances and shell state but cannot see or hear inside. The encounter is never
documented.

## References & Lineage (section 9)

*Katz v. United States* (constitutional anchor); Boullée's *Cenotaph for
Newton* (the monumental sphere); James Turrell (light as material); Doug Wheeler
(anechoic light environments); Tadao Ando and Peter Zumthor (contemplative
material architecture); Orfield Laboratories (the world's quietest room); the
medieval confessional (two-person silence as type).

## Budget & Operations (section 10)

Capital dominated by the custom sphere (acoustic + EM isolation in one
structure), the spiral corridors, and the in-Faraday lighting/control system.
Artwork-only subtotal ~$1.4–3.0M; a custom pavilion adds ~$1.75–2.8M. Annual
operating ~$375–655K/year, dominated by the two-person-on-duty staffing rule.
Throughput ~16 visitors/hour, ~20,000–30,000/year realistic. Three commissioning
paths: permanent museum commission (existing space), biennale (custom pavilion,
6–12 months), standalone institutional commission (dedicated pavilion).

## Open Decisions (section 11)

1. **Site and commissioning path** — which of the three funding models; this
   determines whether a custom pavilion is in scope and whether the work is
   permanent or finite.
2. **Open or enclosed spirals** — whether the corridors are open to the
   spear-field interior (architectural spectacle during the descent) or enclosed
   (the descent is purely anticipation and time). Each produces a different
   register of contemplation.
